El Niño, characterized by warmer ocean temperatures in the central and eastern Pacific, typically leads to increased rainfall in the eastern Pacific and drought conditions in the western Pacific. Conversely, La Niña is marked by cooler ocean temperatures in the same regions, often resulting in opposite weather effects, such as increased rainfall in the western Pacific and drier conditions in the eastern Pacific. These phenomena occur irregularly, approximately every two to seven years, and can last from nine months to two years. Their impacts are felt worldwide, affecting agriculture, water supply, and disaster preparedness. The mechanisms behind El Niño and La Niña are complex, involving interactions between the ocean and atmosphere, and they play a crucial role in shaping seasonal weather patterns across various regions.
